MBKRU People’s Report Card & Government Performance Methodology (Ghana v1)

Collated recommendation for editorial, data, and communications teams. Document version 1.0 — May 2026. MBKRU / mbkru-website.

Canonical public copy is maintained on the live site at /methodology (People’s Report Card — Ghana methodology block). Refresh this export when weights or governance rules change.

1. Purpose

MBKRU exists to strengthen informed citizenship in Ghana’s constitutional democracy (1992 Constitution): voters and communities should see clear, sourced, and comparable information about how MPs perform their parliamentary duties, how accessible and responsive they are to constituents, and how the executive and governing majority deliver on published commitments—without pretending to be the Electoral Commission, Parliament, courts, or law enforcement.

This methodology defines what we measure, what data we accept, how scores are built, how citizen input is used, and what we will never claim.

2. Core principles (non-negotiable)

  1. Separation of roles
    • MP index = representative and parliamentary function (representation, law-making, oversight, constituency linkage).
    • Government / programme index = executive and governing programme delivery (manifesto and policy commitments, national programmes).
    We do not blame MPs for pure executive delivery failures unless the MP’s parliamentary oversight role is in scope and evidence exists.
  2. Evidence ladder
    • Tier A — Official / primary: Parliament of Ghana materials (e.g. Hansard via parliament.gh), parliamentary business lists, official registers, cited manifesto excerpts, gazettes, ministerial press statements where treated as primary for a specific claim.
    • Tier B — Authoritative secondary: Supreme Court / CHRAJ / Auditor-General / reputable parliamentary monitors (e.g. ecosystem around Odekro-style Hansard aggregation) only as labelled secondary with links.
    • Tier C — Citizen experience: structured, moderated MBKRU Voice and related submissions—reported experience, not adjudicated fact.
  3. Non-ideological measurement We score presence, process, transparency, responsiveness, and documented outputs, not whether we agree with a party’s ideology or how an MP votes on value-laden bills—except where we publish a separate, optional “voting record viewer” with no value judgment (fact display only).
  4. Transparency and replicability Every published score lists: definition, formula, source, date pulled, known gaps, and change log between cycles.
  5. Harm reduction Serious allegations require strict moderation; we distinguish opinion, disputed fact, and proven fact; we comply with defamation and data-protection guidance.

3. What makes this “Ghana-style” and unique to MBKRU

Ghana anchors

  • Constitutional duty to attend: Public discourse references Article 97(1)(c) and the 15 sitting-days absence rule as a constitutional expectation. MBKRU uses this only as a published benchmark for interpreting attendance statistics—not as a legal determination of disqualification (that is for Parliament/courts).
  • Hansard-first legislature: Official Hansard is published via Parliament of Ghana; civil society has increased access (e.g. Odekro). MBKRU prefers Hansard-derived metrics where machine access is reliable.
  • Constituency development reality: Ghana’s MP-linked constituency project funding (DACF-related streams) is documented as politicised and often opaque. MBKRU treats constituency project visibility, procurement breadcrumbs, and citizen verification as a Ghana-specific “constituency delivery transparency” pillar.

MBKRU differentiators

  1. Triple ledger: One Legislative Duty Score, one Constituency Accountable Representation Score, one Citizen-Verified Experience Score—then a headline that never hides which ledger moved.
  2. Single citizen pipeline: The same structured MP performance reports feed both journalism/advocacy and the Experience Score, with published triage rules.
  3. Promise bridge: Government performance is explicitly tied to MBKRU-tracked manifesto commitments (dated, status-coded)—aligned with open-government commitment tracking, adapted to Ghana’s manifesto-led electoral cycles.
  4. Ghana fairness rules: Published adjustments for Speaker/Deputy, Majority/Minority leaders, ministers/parliamentary secretaries, long medical leave, by-election mid-term entry, and rural connectivity bias in citizen submissions.

4. Scope: two public products

4.1 People’s Report Card — MP & constituency representation

Units: each ParliamentMember tied to an EC-aligned constituency.

Output: per MP, per published cycle (e.g. half-year or annual): sub-indices + headline + narrative appendix.

4.2 Government performance tracker — programmes and commitments

Units: government programme clusters and/or party manifesto commitments flagged as government programme, plus cross-cutting service delivery themes (health, roads, education, jobs, energy, sanitation).

Output: completion / progress bands with evidence links; prefer theme dashboards before a single composite “president score.”

5. MP methodology: three indices → one headline

5.1 Index A — Legislative Duty (0–100 points)

Intent: Does the MP show up and do the job of a legislator in Parliament?

Structure: Four categories × 25 points = 100; each category may use five sub-metrics × 5 points (adjust to data you can maintain).

Category (25 pts each)Ghana-adapted examples (sub-metrics 0–5)
A1 Plenary & voting presenceSitting attendance; voting participation where roll-call exists; benchmark note vs public/constitutional expectations (context only); exclusions for Speaker/deputies and documented medical leave.
A2 Legislative initiativeBills/motions/questions authored or formally moved (per Hansard/Order Paper); progression where trackable.
A3 Committee & oversightCommittee membership, attendance, chairing; oversight tools (questions, Urgent Statements) if consistently codable.
A4 Integrity of parliamentary conductOnly Tier A/B proven findings (formal sanctions, definitive committee findings)—zero points from raw allegations.

Peer fairness: Use cohort-relative bands (e.g. quartiles vs median MP) where role mix would otherwise distort comparisons.

5.2 Index B — Constituency Accountable Representation (0–50 points)

Intent: Can constituents reach the MP’s office and get meaningful engagement?

Weighting (total 50):

  • 30 points — Availability & constituency infrastructure: constituency office (yes/partial/no; address optional for security); surgeries/town halls frequency; casework capacity; declared outside roles (register of interests where complete); consistent public contact path.
  • 10 points — Parliamentary voice for the constituency: constituency-named interventions in Hansard, adjusted for ministerial speech constraints.
  • 10 points — Constituency project transparency (Ghana-specific): published project lists, budgets, locations, status attributable to MP/constituency role; points for documented public accounting; no points for uncited rumour.

5.3 Index C — Citizen-Verified Experience (0–30 points)

Intent: What do constituents report about responsiveness and respect in real interactions?

Rule: This index measures service experience signals, not criminal guilt or EC outcomes.

Structured fields (minimum): issue category; channel; date range; requested outcome; first response time if known; final status (open/referred/resolved/ignored); optional evidence; consent.

Staff triage codes (publish taxonomy):

  • V1 Verified experience — narrative + corroboration (second report, document, or staff check).
  • V2 Partially supported — plausible, single source, no contradiction.
  • V3 Unsupported / disputed — excluded from scoring; may inform qualitative annex only if ethically cleared.

Aggregation (brigade-resistant): medians and trimmed means; cap per verified cluster; publish response-time distribution and theme mix; optional normalisation by digital access proxy—publish proxy source.

5.4 Headline MP score

Recommended v1 formula:

Headline = 0.50 × A (Legislative Duty) + 0.35 × B (Constituency Representation) + 0.15 × C (Citizen Experience)

Rationale: parliamentary work carries largest weight for credibility; constituency representation matches Ghanaian political practice; citizen experience is meaningful but capped to limit gaming and selection bias.

Display: optional letter bands or colour bands (“Strong / Adequate / Weak / Insufficient data”).

6. Government performance methodology (parallel)

Per commitment or programme item: (1) source artefact; (2) status: Not started → In progress → Substantially complete → Complete / Blocked (with reason); (3) evidence pack (1–3 citations per status change); (4) citizen reports as case evidence only—they do not alone mark a commitment complete.

Dashboards: theme completion rates, stalled items, regional disparity where geotagged reports exist.

7. Publication cycle and governance

  1. Fixed cycles (e.g. biannual MP headline; quarterly component dashboards).
  2. Freeze dates for datasets (Hansard pull, attendance, citizen report window).
  3. Methodology council: review weights annually; no mid-cycle weight changes.
  4. Pre-publication factual dispute window: MPs/staff may contest Tier A factual errors with evidence; no veto over Tier C experience summaries except factual mis-identification (wrong MP/constituency).

8. Ethics, limitations, mandatory disclaimers

  • Selection bias in digital submissions.
  • Constituency inequality; normalise where possible.
  • Coordinated campaigns: rate limits, dedupe, velocity anomalies.
  • Serious allegations: legal review before naming in scored components.
  • Scores are editorial analytics, not legal sanctions.

9. Implementation checklist

  1. Data register: one row per metric → owner → frequency → URL template.
  2. Hansard parser / manual coding SOP for A2/A3/B2 (start narrow).
  3. MP onboarding survey for Index B.
  4. Citizen form rubric + triage UI for staff.
  5. Scorecard cycle objects + immutable cycle methodology snapshot.
  6. Public methodology page + optional downloadable CSV of components for researchers.

10. One-sentence positioning

MBKRU’s Ghana methodology combines Hansard-grounded legislative duty, constituency transparency suited to Ghana’s project-fund politics, and structured citizen experience—merged only with explicit weights—while tracking government delivery against cited manifesto commitments like an open-government commitment register.
