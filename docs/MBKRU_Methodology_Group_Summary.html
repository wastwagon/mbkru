MBKRU methodology — summary for group review

Plain language · May 2026 · Share feedback with the team

For the full Ghana v1 framework (weights, evidence ladder, references), see the public page /methodology on the deployed site.

What this is for

We want citizens to see clearly how MPs do their parliamentary job, how reachable they are to people in the constituency, and how well government delivers on promises we can actually cite — without pretending we are the Electoral Commission, Parliament, or a court.

Two main products

1. People’s Report Card (MPs) — focus on the MP as representative and legislator.

2. Government performance tracker — focus on programmes and manifesto-style commitments we track with sources.

How we stay credible

We split facts from Parliament and official documents from what citizens say happened to them.

We measure showing up, speaking up, following process, being reachable, not whether we like a party’s ideology.

Serious accusations are moderated; we do not treat rumours as proven facts in the score.

The MP score — three parts, then one headline

1. Legislative duty — attendance and participation, bills, motions, and questions where records allow, committee work, oversight tools. Uses Hansard and official records where possible. Integrity here only uses proven findings, not social media allegations.

2. Constituency representation — office, surgeries, casework help, real ways to contact the MP; how often they raise the constituency in Parliament. Ghana-specific: whether constituency projects are explained in public, traceable ways (not gossip).

3. Citizen experience — structured reports from people (MBKRU Voice style), staff-triaged as verified, partly supported, or not usable for scoring. We use medians and caps so coordinated pile-ons do not dominate.

Suggested headline mix (for discussion)

Roughly half legislative duty, just over a third constituency representation, a smaller slice citizen experience — so the score stays anchored in what Parliament records show, while still reflecting real constituent experience.

Government tracker (separate)

Each promise or programme line has a status (not started → in progress → done or blocked) with links to evidence. Citizen reports can illustrate delays or problems but do not by themselves mark something as delivered.

Ghana-specific angles

Public debate on MP attendance and constitutional expectations — we can show attendance in context, not declare someone disqualified.

Constituency project money is often opaque; we reward documented transparency, not who shouts loudest online.

Process the group should weigh in on

Publication cycle — for example, twice a year for headline scores.

Dispute window before publish — MPs can fix clear factual mistakes in the official-data part; they cannot veto honest citizen experience summaries, except when the wrong person or constituency was named.

Annual review of weights and rules — no random mid-cycle changes.

What we need from you (the group)

Is the balance between Parliament data, constituency access, and citizen reports right for Ghana?

What data can we actually get reliably from Parliament and other sources?

What language and disclaimers feel fair to MPs and fair to the public?

Any risks (legal, political, safety) we should add to the public methodology page?

One line to remember

We are building a transparent, Ghana-grounded scorecard: official record where we can, constituency openness where it matters, citizen voice structured and moderated — plus a separate government promise tracker with evidence, not vibes.
